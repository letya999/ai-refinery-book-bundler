Chapter 2

\n

The Decision Filter

\n

A practical framework for cutting through noise and reaching the right answer faster — without missing what matters.

\n
\n\n
\n

Most decisions are not hard

\n

They feel hard because of how we frame them. We collect more information than needed, involve more people than necessary, and set higher bars for certainty than the stakes require.

\n\n

The decision filter is a two-part test applied before any significant choice. It does not guarantee the right answer — nothing does. It does eliminate the wrong process, which is most of the problem.

\n

A practical framework for cutting through noise and reaching the right answer faster — without missing what matters.

\n\n
A fast wrong answer you can correct beats a slow right answer arrived at too late.
\n
\n\n
\n

Part 1: Is this reversible?

\n

Reversible decisions deserve low ceremony. Make them quickly, observe results, adjust. Most decisions you agonize over are reversible. You can change the meeting format. You can refactor the function. You can rewrite the email.

\n\n

Irreversible decisions deserve careful thought proportional to their impact. Hiring, architectural choices, public commitments — these earn extended consideration. Everything else does not.

\n\n
\n How to tell if a decision is reversible\n
\n

Ask: \"If I make this choice and it turns out to be wrong, what does correcting it cost?\" If the answer is measured in hours or days, it is reversible. If measured in months, large money, or damaged relationships, treat it as irreversible.

\n
\n
\n
\n\n
\n

Part 2: Who is actually affected?

\n

Decisions expand to fill the number of stakeholders you invite. Define the real scope first. If the outcome only affects you and your immediate team, make the call. Do not escalate upward by default — escalation signals uncertainty and invites second-guessing.

\n\n
\n
1person should own each decision
\n
≤3people to consult on most choices
\n
24hmaximum deliberation for reversible decisions
\n
\n
\n\n
\n

Applying the filter

\n

Run both tests before scheduling the meeting, writing the document, or building the slide deck. Most of the time, you will find the decision was already made — you just needed permission to act on it.

\n\n

The filter does not replace judgment. It creates the conditions in which judgment can operate quickly and cleanly, without the noise that accumulates when process becomes an end in itself.

\n

We collect more information than needed, involve more people than necessary, and set higher bars for certainty than the stakes require.

Chapter 3

\n

The Feedback Loop

\n

Every system that improves does so through feedback. The question is not whether you have a feedback loop — it is whether you designed it or inherited it by accident.

\n
\n\n
\n

Accidental feedback loops

\n

Most feedback loops in organizations are accidental. They activate too late, arrive through the wrong channel, or measure the wrong signal. A quarterly review is a feedback loop. So is a customer complaint. So is a test suite. The difference is the latency.

\n\n

High-latency feedback produces confident, wrong decisions. You act, weeks pass, the result arrives — but by then the context has changed and the attribution is murky. Was the outcome caused by the decision or by the dozen other things that happened in between?

\n

Every system that improves does so through feedback.

\n\n
The goal is not more feedback. The goal is faster, cleaner signal from the right source at the right time.
\n
\n\n
\n

Designing a tight loop

\n
\n
\n

Act

\n

Make the smallest change that tests your hypothesis. Not the full solution — the probe.

\n
\n
\n

Observe

\n

Define what you will measure before you act. Post-hoc metrics are almost always cherry-picked.

\n
\n
\n

Adjust

\n

Update your model based on what actually happened, not what you wanted to happen.

\n
\n
\n\n

Notice the loop starts with the smallest possible action, not the most comprehensive one. Comprehensive actions are expensive to reverse and expensive to attribute. Small probes are cheap to run and easy to read.

\n
\n\n
\n

The enemy: confirmation

\n

The most dangerous moment in a feedback loop is when the first result confirms what you already believed. Confirmation bias activates instantly and stops you from running the next probe. One data point is not a loop — it is a starting point.

\n\n
\n Signs your feedback loop is broken\n
\n
    \n
  • You measure what is easy to count, not what actually matters
    • \n
  • Results arrive after you have already committed to the next step
    • \n
  • Negative results get reclassified as \"noise\" while positive results get promoted as signal
    • \n
  • The person who made the decision is also the person who evaluates the result
    • \n
\n
\n
\n\n

These patterns feel normal inside them. They require external pressure or deliberate discipline to break. Building the feedback loop before you act — not after — is that discipline.

\n

Most feedback loops in organizations are accidental.
