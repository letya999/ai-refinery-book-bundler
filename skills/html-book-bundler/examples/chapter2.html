Chapter 2

The Decision Filter

A practical framework for cutting through noise and reaching the right answer faster — without missing what matters.

Most decisions are not hard

They feel hard because of how we frame them. We collect more information than needed, involve more people than necessary, and set higher bars for certainty than the stakes require.

The decision filter is a two-part test applied before any significant choice. It does not guarantee the right answer — nothing does. It does eliminate the wrong process, which is most of the problem.

A fast wrong answer you can correct beats a slow right answer arrived at too late.

Part 1: Is this reversible?

Reversible decisions deserve low ceremony. Make them quickly, observe results, adjust. Most decisions you agonize over are reversible. You can change the meeting format. You can refactor the function. You can rewrite the email.

Irreversible decisions deserve careful thought proportional to their impact. Hiring, architectural choices, public commitments — these earn extended consideration. Everything else does not.

How to tell if a decision is reversible

Ask: "If I make this choice and it turns out to be wrong, what does correcting it cost?" If the answer is measured in hours or days, it is reversible. If measured in months, large money, or damaged relationships, treat it as irreversible.

Part 2: Who is actually affected?

Decisions expand to fill the number of stakeholders you invite. Define the real scope first. If the outcome only affects you and your immediate team, make the call. Do not escalate upward by default — escalation signals uncertainty and invites second-guessing.

Applying the filter

Run both tests before scheduling the meeting, writing the document, or building the slide deck. Most of the time, you will find the decision was already made — you just needed permission to act on it.

The filter does not replace judgment. It creates the conditions in which judgment can operate quickly and cleanly, without the noise that accumulates when process becomes an end in itself.
